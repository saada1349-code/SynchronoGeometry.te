\noindent Abstract

\noindent This paper introduces SynchronoGeometry, a novel geometric framework that simultaneously addresses two of the most fundamental open problems in physics: the accelerated expansion of the universe and the quantum measurement problem. We propose that local temporal rhythms at each spacetime point naturally generate cosmic expansion through temporal asynchrony, eliminating the need for dark energy. At the same time, these rhythms provide a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse during quantum measurement. The theory offers rigorous mathematical formulations, operator-level proofs, and testable predictions verifiable through upcoming missions such as JWST, Euclid, and DESI, as well as laboratory quantum experiments. SynchronoGeometry suggests a paradigm shift in our understanding of time, quantum phenomena, and cosmic evolution.

\section{Introduction}
The standard $\Lambda$CDM model, while phenomenologically successful, relies on two mysterious components: dark matter and dark energy. Dark energy, in particular, constitutes nearly

70\% of the universe's energy density yet lacks a fundamental theoretical explanation. Simultaneously, quantum mechanics continues to grapple with the measurement problem-the unexplained transition from quantum superposition to definite outcomes.

SynchronoGeometry addresses both challenges by reinterpreting the nature of time itself. We propose that time is not globally uniform but instead possesses local rhythms that vary across spacetime. These temporal asynchronous naturally reproduce the effects attributed to dark energy while simultaneously providing a physical mechanism for quantum measurement.

\textbf{Theorem 1 (Microcausality for smeared operators).} \textit{Let rhythm operators be defined distributionally so that for any test functions $f,g\in C^{\infty}_{c}(\mathbb{R}^{1+3})$ we have smeared operators}

\[\hat{A}[f]=\int dt\,d^{3}x\,f(t,x)\,\hat{A}(t,x).\]

\textit{If the Fourier transform of the vacuum commutator has support only in the region $\{(\omega,k):|\omega|\geq c|k|\}$, then for any two test functions with spacelike-separated supports:}

\[[\hat{A}[f],\hat{A}[g]]=0.\]

\subsection{Mathematical definition of rhythm operators}
\textbf{Definition 2 (Common domain).} \textit{Assume the self-adjoint family of interaction operators $\{\hat{H}_{int}(t)\}_{t\in\mathbb{R}}$ is defined on a common dense domain $\mathcal{D}_{0}\subset \mathcal{H}$, and for all $\psi\in \mathcal{D}_{0}$, the map $t\mapsto \hat{H}_{int}(t)\psi$ is strongly continuous.}

\textbf{Definition 3 (Applicable domain).} \textit{For a weight function $f\in L^{1}(\mathbb{R})\cap L^{\infty}(\mathbb{R})$ and any $t_{0}\in\mathbb{R}$, define}

\[\mathcal{D}=\left\{\psi\in \mathcal{D}_{0}:\int_{\mathbb{R}}|f(s)|\|\hat{H}_{int }(t_{0}+s)\psi\|ds<\infty\right\}.\]

\textbf{Theorem 2 (Properties of rhythm operators).} \textit{Under the above assumptions, the time-ordered definition}

\[\mathcal{R}(t_{0})=\mathcal{T}\exp\left(-i\int_{\mathbb{R}}f(s)\,\hat{H}_{int}(t_{0}+s)\,ds\right)\]

\textit{is meaningful on $\mathcal{D}$ and closable.}
